TOEFL Academic Discussion (2026): Complete Guide

Updated for the January 2026 TOEFL iBT format · 5 min read

Write for an Academic Discussion is the final task in the 2026 TOEFL Writing section. You read a professor's question and two student responses, then contribute your own response of at least 120 words.

What the Task Looks Like

The screen shows a professor's open-ended academic question, followed by two student responses (named Student A and Student B). You must write a new contribution that adds meaningfully to the conversation.

Example professor question:
"Some people believe universities should focus on preparing students for careers. Others feel they should emphasize broad intellectual development. What is your view and why?"

Student A: "I think career preparation is more important because graduates need jobs..."
Student B: "I disagree. A broad education builds critical thinking that helps in any career..."

What Makes a High-Scoring Response

Top-scoring responses have four elements — all must be present:

  1. Clear opinion: "I believe...", "In my view...", "I would argue that..."
  2. Specific example or reason: "For example...", "Research shows...", "In my experience..."
  3. Reasoning: "This is because...", "Therefore...", "As a result..."
  4. Engagement: Reference a classmate by name — "While [Name] argues X, I believe Y..."

Sample High-Scoring Response

While I understand Maria's point about career readiness, I strongly believe that universities should prioritize broad intellectual development. A narrow focus on vocational skills leaves graduates ill-equipped to adapt when industries change — as we saw when automation transformed manufacturing in the 2010s. For example, students who study philosophy, history, or literature develop analytical and communication skills that transfer across dozens of fields. Furthermore, as Carlos notes, critical thinking is indeed valuable in any career. However, I would add that this thinking is best cultivated through exposure to diverse disciplines rather than a single career track. Universities are uniquely positioned to develop whole people, not just workers. Societies benefit most when graduates can think flexibly and contribute across multiple domains throughout their lives.

Word count: 137 words — references both students, clear opinion, specific example, strong reasoning.

6 Tips for a Top Score

  • State your opinion in the very first sentence — don't build up to it
  • Reference at least one classmate by name — "While [Name] argues..."
  • Include a specific example — general statements score lower than concrete ones
  • Aim for 130-150 words — hitting 120 is the floor, not the target
  • Use discourse markers: Furthermore, However, Therefore, In contrast
  • End with a strong concluding sentence — don't just stop mid-thought
