🎧 TOEFL Listening Practice 2026

Updated for the January 2026 TOEFL iBT format · Covers all 3 listening task types

The TOEFL 2026 Listening section tests your ability to understand spoken English in academic settings. You will hear conversations and lectures, then answer questions that test comprehension, inference, and understanding of the speaker's purpose.

Section Overview

The Listening section lasts approximately 36 minutes and includes:

  • 2 Conversations — between two students, or a student and a campus staff member
  • 1 Campus Situation — practical daily life scenario on campus
  • 3 Academic Lectures — professor-led talks on academic topics, sometimes with student interaction

You can only listen to each audio once. You may take notes while listening — this is strongly recommended.

Task Type 1 — Conversations

What it sounds like

Two people talking — usually two students discussing an assignment, a student and a librarian, or a student and an advisor. Conversations are about 3 minutes long and are followed by 5 questions.

Common question types in Conversations

  • Gist/Purpose: "Why does the student visit the professor?" — answered by identifying the main reason for the interaction
  • Detail: "According to the man, when is the deadline?" — requires noting specific facts
  • Implication: "What does the woman imply when she says...?" — tests inference from tone or indirect language
  • Attitude: "What is the student's attitude toward the assignment?" — listen for tone (excited, worried, frustrated)
Example conversation topic:
A student visits the registrar's office to ask about adding a class late in the semester. The staff member explains the process and asks the student to get a professor's signature. The student is worried about the deadline.

Sample question: "Why does the student visit the registrar's office?"
A) To drop a class B) To add a class late ✓ C) To change her major D) To request a transcript

Task Type 2 — Campus Situations

What it sounds like

A short practical exchange — checking out a book from the library, asking about a campus event, or a brief administrative interaction. About 2 minutes, 3 questions.

Key skills tested

  • Understanding the purpose of the interaction quickly
  • Identifying key information (times, places, requirements)
  • Following a short problem-solution structure

Task Type 3 — Academic Lectures

What it sounds like

A professor gives a 4–5 minute academic lecture on topics from biology, history, psychology, economics, astronomy, art, or linguistics. Sometimes students ask questions. 6 questions follow.

All 6 question types you will see

  • Main Idea: "What is the lecture mainly about?" — identify the central topic, not a detail
  • Detail: "According to the professor, what causes X?" — specific factual answer from the lecture
  • Organization: "Why does the professor mention X?" — understand why a particular example was given
  • Inference: "What can be inferred about Y?" — answer must logically follow from what was said
  • Attitude/Stance: "What is the professor's attitude toward Z?" — tone and word choice reveal the answer
  • Connecting Content: Match information, put steps in order, or classify items — often shown as a table or chart question
Example lecture topic:
A professor discusses the concept of "island biogeography" — how species diversity on islands is determined by island size and distance from the mainland.

Sample questions:
1. "What is the main point of the lecture?" → The relationship between island characteristics and species diversity
2. "According to the professor, what two factors determine species diversity?" → Island size and distance from mainland
3. "Why does the professor mention the Galapagos Islands?" → To provide a well-known example of island biogeography

Note-Taking Strategy

You are allowed to take notes during the listening section. Here is the recommended approach:

  • Write key words, not sentences — there isn't time for full sentences
  • Note the main topic in the first 30 seconds — the professor always states it early
  • Use arrows and abbreviations — e.g. → (leads to), ∴ (therefore), eg. (example), imp. (important)
  • Mark contrast words — "however", "but", "on the other hand" signal important distinctions
  • Star things that sound important — when the professor says "the key point is..." or "importantly..."
  • Note examples with "EG" — example questions often ask why a specific example was given

Topics That Commonly Appear

Academic lectures draw from these subject areas — you do not need specialist knowledge, but familiarity helps:

  • Life Sciences: animal behavior, ecosystems, evolution, biology
  • Physical Sciences: astronomy, geology, chemistry, physics concepts
  • Social Sciences: psychology, economics, sociology, anthropology
  • Arts & Humanities: art history, architecture, literature, music
  • History: historical events, inventions, social movements
